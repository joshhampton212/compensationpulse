Chapter One Free Preview

How to Read & Cite BLS Data in Your Negotiation

Most nurses walk into salary negotiations with a number in their head — something they saw on a salary comparison site, heard from a colleague, or just feels right. The problem: employers know the real data, and you probably don't. That asymmetry costs nurses real money.

The Bureau of Labor Statistics publishes the most authoritative, defensible compensation data available — and it's completely free. Here's how to use it like a professional.

What is OEWS?

The Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics (OEWS) program surveys 1.1 million establishments twice a year. For RNs (SOC code 29-1141), it reports wage percentiles for every state and 400+ metro areas. This is the same data HR departments use to set salary bands.

The three numbers you need to know

Before any negotiation, look up three figures for your metro area on NurseWage or directly at bls.gov:

1
The 50th percentile (median) for your metro
This is the midpoint — half of RNs in your area earn more, half earn less. It's your floor in any negotiation. If you're being offered below this, you have immediate grounds to push back.
2
The 75th percentile for your metro
This is your target. Experienced RNs with specialty skills or certifications should aim here. Framing your ask around the 75th percentile is both assertive and defensible — you're not asking for the top, just the upper quarter.
3
The state mean vs. national mean
If your state's mean is above the national mean of $45.00/hr, that context strengthens your position. If it's below, you can use cost-of-living arguments instead (Chapter 5 covers this for travel comparisons).

How to cite BLS data without sounding rehearsed

The goal isn't to quote statistics robotically — it's to signal that you've done your homework and you know your market value. The most effective framing is matter-of-fact, not adversarial.

Framing tip

Lead with your value, follow with the data. "Based on my experience in the ICU and the current BLS data for this metro, I'm targeting $X" lands better than opening with "BLS says the median is $X."

Chapter Two

Word-for-Word Scripts That Actually Work

Most nurses lose negotiation leverage in the first 60 seconds — not because their ask is unreasonable, but because of how they phrase it. These scripts are built around three principles: confidence without aggression, data as evidence not ultimatum, and always leaving room for a counter.

Script 1 — Initial offer response

"Thank you for the offer. I've been researching compensation for RNs in this market using BLS data, and based on my [X years] of experience in [specialty], I was targeting something closer to $[amount]. Is there flexibility there?"

Use when: You receive the initial written offer and have 24–48 hours to respond.

Chapter Four

Timing — When to Push, When to Accept, When to Walk

Most nurses negotiate at the wrong moment — either too early (before an offer is on the table) or too late (after they've emotionally committed). Leverage has a shelf life. Here's how to use it while it lasts.

1
Before the interview — set your range internally
Look up the BLS percentiles for the metro on NurseWage. Set three numbers in your head: your floor (50th percentile), your ask (75th), and your walk-away (anything below your floor after accounting for total comp). Never share these with the recruiter.
2
During the interview — deflect salary questions
If asked "What are you looking for in terms of salary?", respond with: "I'm flexible — I'd like to hear your range first so I can see if we're in alignment." This keeps your powder dry. Whoever names a number first loses negotiating room.
3
After the verbal offer — this is your best window
The 24–48 hours after a verbal offer is peak leverage. They want you, the job isn't filled yet, and you haven't signed anything. This is when you counter. Don't wait for the written offer — that signals you're deciding between accepting as-is or walking, not negotiating.
4
When to walk
Walk when the final offer is below your floor AND you have reason to believe comparable positions exist at your target wage. Use NurseWage metro data to verify this before declining. Walking on principle without market support is a costly mistake.
Never do this

Don't negotiate via text or email unless you have the offer in writing first. Verbal negotiations leave no record. If a recruiter won't put an offer in writing before asking you to negotiate, that's a red flag about the organization.

The "give me a few days" script

"This is exciting — I'd love a couple of days to review the full offer and make sure I'm giving you a thoughtful answer. Can I get back to you by [specific date]?" This buys time without seeming indecisive, and it gives you space to research comparables.

Chapter Five

Travel Nursing vs. Staff: The Real Compensation Math

Travel nursing packages are deliberately complex — and that complexity benefits agencies, not nurses. A $3,200/week contract sounds dramatically better than an $85,000 staff salary until you do the actual math on taxes, benefits, housing, and career trajectory.

Why the comparison is hard

Travel contracts include a tax-free stipend component (typically housing + meals + incidentals) that isn't actually wages — it's a reimbursement. Only the taxable base pay counts for Social Security, unemployment benefits, mortgage applications, and loan qualification. Agencies aren't required to make this clear.

The real break-even

For most RNs, travel nursing makes financial sense if your total package exceeds your staff equivalent by at least 25–35% — enough to cover the benefits gap, tax complexity, housing carry costs, and career opportunity cost. Run this math for your specific situation before accepting any travel contract.

Using BLS data to negotiate travel contracts

Travel agencies rarely expect nurses to negotiate. Most accept the posted rate. But the posted rate is the agency's opening offer, not a fixed price. The same BLS metro data that helps you negotiate staff positions also gives you leverage here — if the agency's taxable base pay is well below the BLS 25th percentile for that metro, you have grounds to ask for a higher base (even at the expense of a lower stipend, which can be tax-advantaged differently).

Script — Negotiating a travel contract

"I'm really interested in this assignment. I've been looking at BLS wage data for [metro] and the median hourly for RNs is $[X]. Can we look at getting my taxable base closer to that level, even if we adjust the stipend structure? I want to make sure the contract works for my mortgage qualification."

Why this works: framing it around mortgage qualification is both true and non-confrontational. Agencies understand it and often have flexibility they don't volunteer.
